Common English Spelling Errors

English spelling reflects history, sound changes, and borrowed words, so perfect sound-to-letter rules do not exist. A good system combines patterns, word families, and focused review.

1. Frequently confused forms

CorrectCommon errorMemory clue
necessaryneccessaryOne collar (c), two sleeves (ss).
separateseperateThere is “a rat” in sep-a-rat-e.
definitelydefinatelyBuilt from finite.
accommodateaccomodateTwo c’s and two m’s.
governmentgovermentKeep the n from govern.

2. Adding suffixes

A final silent e is often dropped before a vowel suffix: make → making. It is often kept before a consonant suffix: hope → hopeful. A final consonant may double after a short stressed vowel: begin → beginning, but not when the stress is elsewhere: visit → visiting.

Change final consonant + y to i before many suffixes: happy → happiness. Keep y after a vowel: play → played.

3. Homophones and near-homophones

Spell-checkers may not catch a correctly spelled wrong word. Common pairs include their/there/they’re, affect/effect, principal/principle, and stationary/stationery.

Principal can mean the main person or amount; a school principal is your “pal” in the word.

Stationery means writing materials; remember the e in envelope.

4. A five-minute spelling routine

  1. Look at the word and divide it into meaningful chunks.
  2. Say the letters that usually disappear in speech.
  3. Cover the word and write it.
  4. Check it immediately.
  5. Use it in a sentence and review it after one day, three days, and one week.

Review only the letters you miss. Rewriting an easy part ten times wastes effort.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Trusting pronunciation alone.
  • Repeating a misspelling many times while practising.
  • Learning the isolated word without its related forms, such as pronounce/pronunciation.
