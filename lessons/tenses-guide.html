English Tenses: Meaning, Form, and Use

English tense choice is not only about time. It also shows whether an action is complete, ongoing, repeated, connected to the present, or viewed from another point in time.

1. Present forms

FormMain useExample
Present simpleHabit, fact, scheduleShe studies every evening.
Present continuousAction happening now or temporary situationShe is studying now.
Present perfectPast action connected to the presentShe has finished the chapter.
Present perfect continuousOngoing activity up to nowShe has been studying for two hours.

Use the present perfect when the exact past time is not the focus. “I have visited Cox’s Bazar” describes experience. “I visited Cox’s Bazar in 2024” uses the past simple because a finished time is stated.

2. Past forms

The past simple describes a finished event: “The class started at nine.” The past continuous gives background or an interrupted action: “We were studying when the lights went out.”

The past perfect makes an earlier past action clear: “The train had left before we reached the station.” Use it when the order is not already obvious. The past perfect continuous emphasizes duration before a past point: “They had been waiting for an hour before the bus arrived.”

3. Future meaning

English expresses the future in several ways. Will is common for predictions, instant decisions, and promises. Be going to often expresses a prior plan or a prediction based on present evidence. The present continuous is common for arrangements, while the present simple is used for schedules.

Prediction: I think it will rain.

Evidence: Look at those clouds; it is going to rain.

Arrangement: I am meeting the tutor tomorrow.

Schedule: The exam begins at 10:00.

4. A reliable decision process

  1. Locate the time reference: now, a finished past period, or a future point.
  2. Decide whether the action is complete, repeated, temporary, or continuing.
  3. Check whether another action creates a reference point.
  4. Choose the form and verify the auxiliary verb.

Time expressions help, but they are not absolute rules. Words such as always often appear with the present simple, yet “You are always losing your keys” uses the continuous form to express annoyance.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Using the present perfect with a finished time expression such as yesterday.
  • Forgetting the past participle after has, have, or had.
  • Using stative verbs such as know or believe in continuous forms without a special meaning.
Self-check

Test the key ideas

I ___ this book last week.

Answer: bought

She ___ here since January.

Answer: has lived / has been living

By the time we arrived, the meeting ___.

Answer: had ended
