How to Build Vocabulary That You Can Use

Knowing a word means more than recognising a translation. Useful vocabulary knowledge includes pronunciation, grammar, common partners, tone, and the ability to recall the word when speaking or writing.

1. Select high-value words

Choose words that appear repeatedly in your reading, studies, work, or target exams. A smaller set learned deeply is more valuable than a huge list reviewed once. Record the sentence where you found the word; context helps memory and shows natural usage.

2. Build a complete word card

A useful card includes the headword, a simple definition, one original sentence, a common collocation, a related form, and a synonym or antonym.

Word: maintain (verb)

Meaning: keep something in a good or continuing state

Collocations: maintain quality, maintain contact

Family: maintenance

Sentence: A weekly review helps me maintain my progress.

3. Use active recall and spaced review

Do not only reread. Hide the answer and try to retrieve it. Review difficult words more often and easy words less often. A simple schedule is the same day, the next day, three days later, one week later, and two weeks later.

MCQs are useful for recognition, but add production practice: complete a sentence, explain the word without translating it, or write a short paragraph containing three new words.

4. Notice collocations and register

Fluent English depends on frequent combinations such as make a decision, heavy rain, and highly effective. Also note whether a word is formal, informal, technical, approving, or critical.

When you meet a familiar word in a new pattern, update the card. Vocabulary knowledge grows through repeated, varied encounters rather than one perfect definition.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Trying to learn too many unrelated words in one session.
  • Using only bilingual translation and ignoring grammar or collocation.
  • Reviewing by rereading instead of testing recall.
Self-check

Test the key ideas

What should a complete word card include besides a definition?

Answer: Context, collocation, word family, and an original sentence.

Why are MCQs alone insufficient?

Answer: They train recognition more than independent recall.

What is spaced review?

Answer: Reviewing at increasing intervals over time.
