Parts of Speech: A Practical Guide

Every English sentence is built from a small set of word roles. Learning these roles makes grammar easier because you can explain why a word is used, not only whether it sounds correct.

1. Nouns and pronouns

A noun names a person, place, thing, or idea: teacher, Dhaka, book, honesty. A pronoun replaces a noun so that we do not repeat it: Rina bought a book. She read it.

Pronouns must agree with the nouns they replace. In “The students submitted their work,” their correctly refers to the plural noun students.

Quick check: In “Patience helps us solve problems,” Patience is an abstract noun and us is a pronoun.

2. Verbs and adverbs

A verb shows an action, an event, or a state: run, happened, seem, is. A complete sentence normally needs a finite verb. An adverb modifies a verb, adjective, another adverb, or the whole sentence.

SentenceRole of the adverb
She spoke softly.Modifies the verb spoke.
It is very useful.Modifies the adjective useful.
He arrived quite early.Modifies the adverb early.

Not every adverb ends in -ly. Words such as fast, often, here, and yesterday can also function as adverbs.

3. Adjectives and determiners

An adjective describes a noun or pronoun: a difficult question, the sky is blue. Determiners come before nouns and show which one, whose, or how many: this lesson, my pen, several students.

A common mistake is to use an adjective where an adverb is needed. Compare “She is a careful driver” with “She drives carefully.” The first describes the noun driver; the second modifies the verb drives.

4. Prepositions, conjunctions, and interjections

A preposition shows a relationship in time, place, direction, or another idea: at noon, under the table, toward the gate. A conjunction connects words or clauses: and, but, because, although. An interjection expresses a sudden feeling: Wow! Oh! Alas!

When you analyse a sentence, identify the main verb first. Then find the subject, the words that describe it, and the connectors that join ideas. This order is usually faster than trying to label every word from left to right.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Calling every word ending in -ly an adverb; friendly and lovely are usually adjectives.
  • Treating articles a, an, the as adjectives in every modern grammar system; they are more precisely determiners.
  • Confusing a word’s dictionary meaning with its role in a particular sentence. The same word may perform different roles.
Self-check

Test the key ideas

Which word is an adjective in “The narrow road became dangerous”?

Answer: narrow

What part of speech is “because”?

Answer: conjunction

Which word modifies “answered” in “He answered politely”?

Answer: politely
