Subject–Verb Agreement Made Simple

Subject–verb agreement means that the form of a verb must match its subject. The rule is simple in basic sentences, but phrases between the subject and verb often cause mistakes.

1. Find the true subject

Ignore descriptive phrases between the subject and the verb. In “The list of new words is on the desk,” the subject is list, not words. Because list is singular, the verb is is.

Method: Cross out the prepositional phrase mentally: “The list of new words is on the desk.”

2. Present-simple endings

In the present simple, a third-person singular subject normally takes a verb ending in -s or -es: She writes; the machine works. Plural subjects and the pronouns I and you use the base form: They write; I work.

He / she / itplaysShe plays chess.
I / you / we / theyplayThey play chess.
Singular nounneedsThe plan needs revision.
Plural nounneedThe plans need revision.

3. Joined and indefinite subjects

Subjects joined by and are usually plural: “Rafi and Mina are ready.” With either…or and neither…nor, the verb normally agrees with the nearer subject: “Neither the teachers nor the principal is available”; “Neither the principal nor the teachers are available.”

Indefinite pronouns such as everyone, each, anybody, someone, and neither are normally singular: “Everyone has a ticket.” Words such as many, several, and both are plural.

4. Quantities and collective nouns

A measurement viewed as one total often takes a singular verb: “Ten kilometres is a long walk.” Fractions and percentages agree with the noun after of: “Half of the water is gone,” but “Half of the students are absent.”

Collective nouns such as team or committee can be singular when acting as one unit. In many international and American contexts, “The team is winning” is standard. British usage more often allows a plural verb when members are considered separately.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Choosing the verb according to the nearest noun instead of the true subject.
  • Forgetting that news, mathematics, and some other words ending in -s are usually singular.
  • Using a plural verb after each or every: “Every student has,” not “Every student have.”
Self-check

Test the key ideas

The quality of these products ___ improved.

Answer: has

Neither the manager nor the workers ___ available.

Answer: are

Twenty minutes ___ enough for this task.

Answer: is
