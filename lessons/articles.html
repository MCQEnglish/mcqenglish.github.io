Articles: A, An, The, and Zero Article

Articles show whether a noun is general, newly introduced, or already identifiable. Correct article choice depends on meaning and sound, not only on spelling.

1. A and an

Use a or an with a singular countable noun when the listener does not yet know which one: “I saw a bird.” Choose between them by sound. Use an before a vowel sound: an apple, an hour, an honest person. Use a before a consonant sound: a university, a European country, a one-day event.

2. The for identifiable nouns

Use the when both writer and reader can identify the noun. The reference may have been mentioned before, may be unique in context, or may be defined by a phrase.

I bought a notebook. The notebook has a blue cover.

Please close the door. (The relevant door is clear in the situation.)

The student who won the prize is absent.

3. No article for general meaning

Plural countable nouns and uncountable nouns usually take no article when speaking generally: “Books develop imagination”; “Water is essential.” Use the when the reference becomes specific: “The books on this shelf are new”; “The water in this bottle is cold.”

We also commonly use no article with languages, school subjects, meals, sports, and many proper names: English, mathematics, breakfast, cricket, Bangladesh.

4. Names and fixed patterns

Use the with many rivers, seas, oceans, mountain ranges, groups of islands, and plural country names: the Padma, the Bay of Bengal, the Himalayas, the United States. Most cities, individual mountains, lakes, streets, and single countries take no article.

Fixed institutional expressions depend on meaning. “She goes to school” focuses on the normal purpose of school. “She went to the school to meet the head teacher” refers to a particular building.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Selecting a or an from the first written letter rather than the first sound.
  • Using the before a plural noun that has general meaning.
  • Leaving a singular countable common noun without a determiner: say “I need a pen” or “I need the pen,” not “I need pen.”
