Prepositions of Time, Place, and Movement

Prepositions are short words, but they carry precise relationships. Memorising isolated lists helps less than understanding the basic image behind each choice.

1. At, on, and in for time

PrepositionTypical ideaExamples
atExact pointat 7:30, at noon, at night
onDay or dateon Monday, on 21 July
inLonger periodin July, in 2026, in winter

Some phrases are idiomatic: in the morning, but on Monday morning; at the weekend is common in British English, while on the weekend is common in American English.

2. At, on, and in for place

Think of at as a point, on as a surface, and in as an enclosed area. We meet at the gate, put a phone on the table, and sit in a room. Real usage includes fixed patterns, so context matters: a person may be at school as an institution and in the school as a building.

3. Movement and direction

To marks a destination: “We walked to the station.” Into emphasizes movement from outside to inside, while onto emphasizes movement to a surface. Toward gives direction without guaranteeing arrival.

The child ran into the room.

The cat jumped onto the chair.

They drove toward the river but turned back.

4. Since, for, by, and until

Use since for a starting point and for for a duration: “since 2022,” “for four years.” Use by for a deadline, meaning no later than; use until for continuation up to a time.

“Submit the form by Thursday” allows submission earlier. “The office is open until Thursday” means the situation continues and then ends.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Adding to after verbs that do not require it: “discuss the issue,” not “discuss about the issue.”
  • Using since with a duration: say “for three days,” not “since three days.”
  • Translating prepositions word for word from another language; English combinations often need to be learned as chunks.
Self-check

Test the key ideas

The class starts ___ 9:00.

Answer: at

She has worked here ___ five years.

Answer: for

Please finish the work ___ Friday.

Answer: by
